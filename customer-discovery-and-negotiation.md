# Customer Discovery: Asking Questions That Reveal Real Needs

Distilled from 10 foundational books, this skill turns any conversation with a consumer/customer into a reliable source of truth about their needs — across cultures and languages.

**Source library:** The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick) · SPIN Selling (Rackham) · Lean Customer Development (Alvarez) · Talking to Humans (Constable) · A More Beautiful Question (Berger) · Humble Inquiry (Schein) · The Coaching Habit (Stanier) · Gap Selling (Keenan) · Nonviolent Communication (Rosenberg) · The Culture Map (Meyer)

## Part 1 — The Core Rules of Good Questions

### Rule 1: Talk about their life, not your idea (The Mom Test)
People lie to be nice. Never pitch, then ask "would you use this?" Instead:
- Ask about **specifics in the past**, not opinions about the future. "When did this last happen? Walk me through it."
- Bad: "Would you pay for X?" → Good: "What are you currently paying/doing to solve this?"
- Compliments, fluff ("I'd definitely use that"), and hypotheticals are **not data**. Facts, commitments, and money are.
- If they haven't tried to solve the problem themselves, they don't care enough about it.

### Rule 2: Follow the SPIN progression (SPIN Selling)
Sequence questions from context to consequence:
1. **Situation** — facts about their current setup (use sparingly; do homework first)
2. **Problem** — difficulties, dissatisfactions ("What's frustrating about...?")
3. **Implication** — consequences of the problem ("What does that delay cost you downstream?")
4. **Need-payoff** — let *them* articulate the value ("How would it help if...?")
Implication questions are where amateurs stop and experts dig. The customer convincing themselves beats you convincing them.

### Rule 3: Find the gap (Gap Selling)
Every need is a gap between **current state** and **desired future state**. Map both explicitly:
- Current: literal facts, the problem, its impact (technical, business, emotional)
- Future: what "solved" looks like in their words, and what it would mean for them personally
- No gap = no need = no sale. Quantify the gap wherever possible.

### Rule 4: Stay humble and curious (Humble Inquiry)
Ask from a position of genuine not-knowing, not interrogation:
- "Here-and-now humility": they know their life; you don't.
- Prefer open questions you truly don't know the answer to.
- Avoid disguised statements ("Don't you think that...?") — they're telling, not asking.

### Rule 5: Use the seven power questions (The Coaching Habit)
- "What's on your mind?" (opener)
- "And what else?" (the best follow-up in the world — ask it 2–3 times)
- "What's the real challenge here **for you**?" (personalizes and focuses)
- "What do you want?" · "How can I help?" · "If you say yes to this, what are you saying no to?" · "What was most useful for you?"

### Rule 6: Question the question (A More Beautiful Question)
For deeper insight, walk the ladder: **Why?** → **What if?** → **How?**
"Why do you do it that way?" often reveals the assumption everyone stopped seeing.

### Rule 7: Interview like a researcher (Lean Customer Development, Talking to Humans)
- Ask about **behavior**, not intent: "Tell me about the last time you…"
- One question at a time; then be silent. Silence does the digging.
- Get stories, not summaries. "Walk me through your day/that purchase/that failure."
- Look for workarounds — spreadsheets, sticky notes, hired help. Workarounds = validated pain.
- End with: "Who else should I talk to?" and "What should I have asked that I didn't?"

## Part 2 — Emotional Attunement (respond like a human, not a survey)

From Nonviolent Communication and interviewing practice. Needs live under emotions; if you skip the emotion, you get the sanitized answer.

**The empathy loop — use it whenever emotion surfaces:**
1. **Notice** the feeling (tone shift, sigh, laughter, hesitation, strong word choice)
2. **Reflect** it briefly and tentatively: "That sounds genuinely exhausting — is that right?"
3. **Validate** without judging or fixing: "That makes sense given what you're juggling."
4. **Then** deepen: "What's the hardest part of that for you?"

**Rules of humane conversation:**
- Never rush past pain to your next scripted question. When they reveal frustration, that IS the interview — stay there.
- Empathy ≠ agreement, and ≠ pity. It's accurate understanding, reflected back.
- Match energy: mirror their formality, pace, and vocabulary. Use their exact words for their problem, not your jargon.
- Small human reactions are allowed and valuable: "Oof, three hours every week?", "Ha — I've done the same thing."
- Distinguish **feelings** ("I'm overwhelmed") from **needs** ("I need predictability"). Name the need back to them and watch for the relief of feeling understood — that's your confirmation signal.
- Don't fake emotion. One genuine reaction beats five performed ones.

## Part 3 — Multicultural & Multilingual Adaptation (The Culture Map)

Assume nothing transfers. Calibrate on four dials early in the conversation:

| Dial | Low-context (US, DE, NL, Scandinavia) | High-context (JP, CN, IN, FR, Arab world, LATAM) |
|---|---|---|
| Communication | Take words at face value | Read pauses, hedges, what's *not* said |
| Disagreement | "No" is said directly | "That could be difficult" often means no |
| Trust | Built through competence/tasks | Built through relationship/time — invest in small talk first |
| Hierarchy | Anyone speaks freely | Junior people may not voice problems in front of seniors — ask 1:1 |

**Multilingual practice:**
- Speak in short sentences, no idioms, no phrasal-verb stacking ("figure out", "put up with").
- Confirm understanding by asking them to describe it back — never "Do you understand?" (everyone says yes).
- Offer to continue in their stronger language if you can; people describe pain most accurately in their mother tongue.
- In indirect cultures, replace blunt questions with third-person framing: "How do people in your team usually handle...?" — it's face-saving and gets truer answers.
- Watch for "yes" that means "I hear you" rather than "I agree" (common in high-context cultures). Verify with behavioral questions: "What would you do next?"

## Part 4 — Ready-to-Run Interview Flow (any niche)

1. **Open (2 min):** thank them, set frame — "I'm trying to learn, not sell. There are no wrong answers." Relationship-first cultures: extend warm-up.
2. **Context:** "Tell me about your role / your typical day around [topic]."
3. **Problem discovery:** "What's the hardest part about ___?" → "Tell me about the last time that happened." → "And what else?"
4. **Impact (SPIN implication):** "What does that end up costing you — time, money, stress?"
5. **Current solutions:** "What have you tried? What do you love/hate about it? What do you pay?"
6. **Desired future (Gap):** "If this were magically solved, what would be different for you?"
7. **Commitment test (Mom Test):** ask for something that costs them — time, intro, pre-order, pilot. Words are cheap; commitment is data.
8. **Close:** "What should I have asked?" / "Who else should I talk to?"

## Part 5 — Red Flags Checklist (self-audit after every conversation)

- [ ] Did I pitch before learning? (fail)
- [ ] Did I collect compliments instead of facts? (fail)
- [ ] Do I have at least 2 specific past stories? 
- [ ] Do I know what they currently pay/do for this?
- [ ] Did I identify the emotion AND the underlying need?
- [ ] Did I adapt directness/formality to their culture?
- [ ] Did I get a commitment or next step?

# Negotiation Tactics: The Synthesis of 20 Books

## Part 1 — Preparation (where 70% of negotiations are won)

**From Negotiation Genius, Bargaining for Advantage, 3-D Negotiation:**
- **BATNA** (Getting to Yes): know your best alternative if talks fail. Your power = your BATNA, not your desire. Improve it *before* negotiating. Estimate theirs too.
- **Reservation point & ZOPA:** your walk-away number; the zone of possible agreement between both walk-aways.
- **Target high, justify it:** ambitious-but-defensible anchors capture more value. First offers anchor the whole range.
- **3-D moves (Lax & Sebenius):** if the table looks bad, change the setup — add parties, change the sequence, bring in issues that let you trade. Negotiate the deal design, not just the tactics.
- **Ask yourself 10 questions of self-diagnosis (Ask for More):** What do I actually need? What do I feel? What have I assumed?
- **Multiple issues > one issue:** never negotiate price alone. Add timing, scope, volume, guarantees, publicity — so you can trade what's cheap for you but valuable to them (**logrolling**).

## Part 2 — Opening & Frame Control

- **Frame first (Pitch Anything):** whoever's frame holds, wins. Position yourself as the prize, not the supplicant. Keep status through scarcity and time constraints — never chase.
- **Pre-suasion (Cialdini):** what you make salient right before the ask shapes the answer. Open with the theme you want them anchored on (partnership, quality, urgency).
- **Anchor smart (Dawson, Malhotra):** open beyond your target ("bracketing") but within reason; never accept the first offer (it signals they left value on the table and triggers regret on both sides); **flinch** visibly at their first number.
- **Start with No (Camp):** invite "no" early — "Feel free to tell me this doesn't fit." It removes pressure, kills neediness (the #1 killer of deals), and produces honest positions.

## Part 3 — The Conversation: Tactical Empathy & Information

**From Never Split the Difference — the core toolkit:**
- **Mirroring:** repeat their last 1–3 words as a question. They elaborate; you learn.
- **Labeling:** "It seems like you're worried about the timeline." Naming emotions defuses negatives and reinforces positives.
- **Accusation audit:** preempt their objections yourself ("You're probably thinking we're too small/expensive…"). Takes the sting out.
- **Calibrated questions:** open "How/What" questions that make them solve your problem: "How am I supposed to do that?" · "What about this works for you?" Never "Why" (sounds accusatory).
- **Aim for "That's right"** (they feel fully understood) — not "You're right" (they want you to stop talking).
- **Late-night FM DJ voice:** slow, calm, downward-inflected when tension spikes.

**From Getting to Yes / Getting Past No:**
- Separate the **people** from the **problem**; be soft on people, hard on the problem.
- Negotiate **interests, not positions** — ask "why do you want that?" behind every demand.
- When attacked: **go to the balcony** (pause, don't react), then **step to their side** (agree wherever you can), then **reframe** attacks as attacks on the problem.
- **Build them a golden bridge:** make yes easy, let victory look like their idea, help them save face.
- Use **objective criteria** (market rates, precedent, benchmarks) so it's standards vs. standards, not will vs. will.

**From Difficult Conversations / Crucial Conversations / Beyond Reason:**
- Every hard talk is three conversations: what happened, feelings, identity. Address all three.
- Start from the **third story** — how a neutral observer would describe the disagreement.
- Make it **safe**: people go silent or violent when safety is lost. Restore it with contrast statements: "I don't mean X; I do mean Y."
- Feed the five core concerns (Beyond Reason): **appreciation, affiliation, autonomy, status, role.** Most blowups are one of these starving.

**From Getting More (Diamond):**
- People are the point: value their perceptions, use their standards against their positions ("You say you value fairness — is this fair?").
- Trade items of unequal value; be incremental; small steps beat big leaps with distrustful parties.

## Part 4 — Persuasion Levers (Influence, To Sell Is Human)

Cialdini's principles, applied ethically:
1. **Reciprocity** — give first (info, concession, favor), and label your concessions so they're recognized.
2. **Commitment/consistency** — get small yeses in writing/publicly; they compound.
3. **Social proof** — reference similar customers/deals.
4. **Liking** — genuine similarity and compliments move numbers.
5. **Authority** — credentials and trade-record, shown not told.
6. **Scarcity** — real deadlines and limited capacity; never fake it.

Pink's ABCs: **Attunement** (take their perspective, lower your power feel), **Buoyancy** (interrogative self-talk, positivity ratio), **Clarity** (frame the problem, not just the solution; make the ask specific with an off-ramp).

## Part 5 — Concessions, Deadlock & Closing (Dawson, Wheeler, Camp)

- **Never concede without getting something.** "If I do X, can you do Y?" — always trade, never gift.
- **Decreasing increments:** each concession smaller than the last; signals you're near bottom.
- **Higher authority:** "I need to check with my partner/board" buys time and deflects pressure. Neutralize theirs early: "If this works for you, is there any reason you couldn't decide today?"
- **Good guy/bad guy, nibbling, the vise ("You'll have to do better than that")** — recognize these classics and name them calmly to defuse them.
- **Deadlock tools:** change a variable (people, place, timeline, spec), set the hard issue aside, use a recess, or bring objective criteria.
- **Improvise (Wheeler):** plans die on contact; negotiate like jazz — learn, adapt, keep the critical moments (openings, closings, turning points) deliberate.
- **Close:** summarize agreements in their words, confirm implementation details (who/what/when), get it in writing same day. A deal without execution terms is a wish.

## Part 6 — Multicultural & Multilingual Negotiation (Culture Map; Kiss, Bow, or Shake Hands)

Calibrate these dials before you pick tactics:

| Dial | If counterpart is… | Adapt by… |
|---|---|---|
| **Trust basis** | Relationship-first (Asia, LATAM, Middle East, Africa, Southern EU) | Invest in meals/small talk over multiple meetings before terms; rushing = disrespect |
| | Task-first (US, DE, NL, UK, Scandinavia) | Get to business quickly; over-socializing seems evasive |
| **Disagreement style** | Confrontational (FR, DE, IL, NL) | Debate is engagement, not hostility — don't fold at pushback |
| | Avoids-confrontation (JP, TH, MX, ID) | Soften "no", read hedges ("difficult", "we will consider") as no, allow face-saving exits |
| **Hierarchy** | Hierarchical (JP, KR, CN, IN, RU, Arab world) | Match seniority levels at the table; the real decider may be absent — ask about the decision process |
| **Contracts** | Relationship cultures | The signed contract is a milestone, not the end; expect renegotiation |
| | Rule cultures | The contract is sacred; renegotiating reads as bad faith |
| **Time** | Linear (DE, CH, US, JP) | Punctuality and agendas are respect |
| | Flexible (LATAM, Middle East, South Asia) | Build slack; agenda-forcing backfires |

**Multilingual practice:**
- Short sentences, no idioms, no sarcasm (it does not translate). Numbers in writing, always — spoken numbers across accents are a classic source of deal-killing errors.
- Summarize agreements in writing after each session in simple language; ask them to confirm in their own words.
- Silence: in East Asia and Finland it means thinking, not discomfort. Do not fill it — filling silence is where people concede unnecessarily.
- If using interpreters: brief them beforehand, speak to the counterpart (not the interpreter), pause often.
- Humor, especially irony, is high-risk across cultures; warmth and patience are universal.
