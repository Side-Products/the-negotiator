# Hack-Nation Judge Research and Moha Shah Mentor Call Playbook

**Research date:** 18 July 2026<br>
**Prepared by:** Claus<br>
**Challenge:** ElevenLabs — The Negotiator<br>
**Current product direction:** FairFix, an evidence-based negotiation system for car-repair quotes

## Executive conclusion

The current Hack-Nation jury is not published as a complete public roster. One current judge can be confirmed with high confidence: **Maksym Petyak**. No current ElevenLabs-specific judge could be verified publicly.

The historical jury is large, international, and often track-specific. There is no evidence that teams should optimize for a single US or MIT-style preference. The strongest recurring judging pattern is:

1. a real and immediately understandable problem;
2. a technically complete product loop;
3. originality beyond a model or voice wrapper;
4. trustworthy behavior and visible validation;
5. a concise, memorable demonstration.

For The Negotiator, a generic product that calls three providers and asks for a better price is not sufficiently differentiated. A previous ElevenLabs winner already did provider discovery, parallel calls, comparison, booking, and competitive price negotiation.

The recommended positioning is therefore:

> **FairFix is a multilingual repair-quote verification and negotiation system. It converts vehicle documents and inspection reports into one confirmed repair specification, obtains comparable itemized quotes, detects missing or misleading line items, negotiates with truthful evidence, and proves exactly what changed.**

Moha Shah is most valuable to the team as a **VC, go-to-market, corporate-partnership, mobility, and insurtech sparring partner**. The mentor call should force decisions about the initial buyer and distribution wedge, not become a broad product brainstorm or technical review.

## 3. What Hack-Nation judges demonstrably reward

### Official scoring dimensions

The official judging walkthrough uses three equally important dimensions, each scored from one to five:

1. **Technical depth** — complexity, implementation, and engineering quality.
2. **Communication and presentation** — clarity of explanation, documentation, and demonstration.
3. **Innovation and creativity** — originality and whether the project genuinely stands out.

Judges inspect the project description, videos, and repository. Inaccessible code or videos cannot be evaluated properly. [Official Hack-Nation judging guide](https://www.loom.com/share/77ffd85173f14080b77832694636f65a)

Market size and social impact can strengthen the story, but they are not standalone formal scoring categories. They should not displace engineering completeness or demonstration quality.

## 4. Previous winners and the differentiation risk

The fourth-edition ElevenLabs results were:

1. CallPilott
2. SignCall
3. OmniCallAI

[Official ElevenLabs track results](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/hack-nation_hacknation-conversationalai-elevenlabs-activity-7429494843460005888-ZlcE)

### OmniCallAI is the key precedent

Its public description already includes:

- provider discovery;
- parallel voice agents;
- service booking;
- provider comparison;
- competitive price negotiation;
- calendar and route optimization.

[Creator description](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/muhammad-arslan-toor_ai-hackathon-elevenlabs-activity-7426945592922112001-LFgX)

Therefore, this is too generic:

> Our AI calls three providers and negotiates the best price.

The defensible innovation is the **verified negotiation loop**:

- one confirmed and versioned specification;
- identical requirements for every provider;
- itemized and genuinely comparable quotes;
- detection of missing scope and misleading lowball prices;
- truthful leverage rather than fabricated bids;
- transcript evidence showing what caused a better price or term;
- explicit human approval before commitment.

### Social impact must be built into the mechanism

SignCall already used sign-language video input and an autonomous phone agent to help Deaf and non-speaking users book medical appointments. [Team description](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/bethany-anne-wong_recently-our-team-won-2nd-place-in-the-elevenlabs-activity-7430685496898654208-EZ5L)

For FairFix, multilingual intake, captions, plain-language quote explanations, and transparent user control are credible impact mechanisms. A general claim that the product “helps disadvantaged people” is not enough by itself.

## 5. Recommended FairFix submission

### One-line pitch

> FairFix turns vehicle documents and inspection reports into one verified repair specification, obtains comparable workshop quotes, detects hidden scope differences, and negotiates price, warranty, and timing with a complete evidence trail.

### Judging-critical demo flow

1. Upload a vehicle document, inspection report, or existing repair quote.
2. Extract one structured repair specification.
3. Read the specification back to the user and correct it if necessary.
4. Freeze and version the confirmed specification.
5. Give the same specification to three different workshop counter-agents.
6. Capture itemized quote objects and transcript evidence.
7. Detect missing VAT, parts, labor, diagnostics, disposal, warranty, or timing terms.
8. Use one real comparable offer as truthful leverage with another provider.
9. Show the exact turn that improved price, warranty, appointment, or mobility terms.
10. Rank the final offers and require human approval before booking.

### What the judges should remember

> **Same repair. Three comparable quotes. €1,240 before, €910 after. Every difference and every negotiation claim is verifiable.**

The amounts are illustrative until replaced by the team’s real demo data.

### Current vertical ranking

1. **Car-repair quote verification — recommended.** Best balance of technical depth, instant comprehension, evidence, social usefulness, and a memorable before-and-after result.
2. **Equipment rental for small contractors.** Strong B2B economics and procurement structure, but harder to explain quickly to a generalist panel.
3. **Funeral price-rights advocate.** Memorable and socially meaningful but ethically delicate and more US-specific.
4. **Narrow urgent freight.** Credible only with authentic documents and a very constrained scenario.
5. **Movers.** Safest fit with the challenge example but least differentiated.

## 6. Moha Shah mentor call strategy

### Why Moha is unusually useful to this project

Moha’s highest-value experience for the team is not voice architecture. It is:

- venture-capital evaluation and investment-committee thinking;
- portfolio development and startup go-to-market;
- mobility and insurtech;
- construction technology;
- Fortune 100 innovation and digital transformation;
- corporate partnerships and pilot design;
- product leadership and startup positioning.

She can help decide whether the strongest venture wedge is:

- direct-to-consumer FairFix;
- B2B2C distribution through an insurer, automobile club, leasing company, or employee-benefit partner;
- a recurring fleet workflow;
- or equipment rental for small contractors.

### Required outcomes

Leave the call with decisions or explicit hypotheses for:

1. initial vertical;
2. first ideal customer profile;
3. user, economic buyer, and internal champion;
4. one primary value metric;
5. credible business model;
6. shortest European pilot route;
7. the single most convincing hackathon proof point;
8. largest investor or enterprise objection;
9. one next customer-interview profile or introduction, if appropriate.

### 30-minute agenda

| Time | Topic | Desired output |
|---:|---|---|
| 0–2 min | State the purpose and give the short pitch | Agreement on the decisions the call should produce |
| 2–5 min | Show a maximum 60-second product flow and current assumptions | Shared context without retelling the challenge |
| 5–12 min | Force a choice among vertical, ICP, user, and buyer | One preferred wedge and one fallback |
| 12–19 min | GTM, channel, paid pilot, and business model | Fastest credible route to customers |
| 19–25 min | Investment-committee and judge red team | Fatal objection and missing proof |
| 25–28 min | Trust, incentives, and social-impact mechanics | One necessary guardrail and one credible impact mechanism |
| 28–30 min | Repeat decisions and agree next step | Named actions, owner, and deadline |

### Opening pitch

> We’re building FairFix, an AI negotiation agent for car repairs. It converts vehicle documents and inspection reports into one confirmed repair specification, obtains comparable itemized quotes, detects misleading or missing line items, and negotiates price, warranty, and timing using verifiable evidence. We’d like your help choosing the strongest initial buyer and fastest go-to-market wedge in Europe.

Follow immediately with:

> By the end of this call, we’d love to leave with one clear ICP, buyer, pilot route, and the single proof point our demo must establish.

### Questions to ask in priority order

#### Must ask

1. **Given your mobility, insurance, and construction-tech experience, which wedge would you back: consumers negotiating car repairs, small fleets doing this repeatedly, or contractors negotiating equipment rental—and why?**

2. **For your preferred wedge, who is the user, who signs the contract, who champions it internally, and which budget or KPI pays for it?**

3. **What would make this an urgent purchase rather than a useful feature?**

4. **What is the shortest credible route to a paid European pilot: mobility clubs, insurers, fleets, leasing companies, brokers, marketplaces, or another channel?**

5. **Where do you see incentive conflicts—for example, would an insurer or AAA-like partner welcome lower repair costs, or resist an agent negotiating against its existing provider network?**

6. **Red-team us as an investment committee: what is the fatal objection—low purchase frequency, customer-acquisition cost, liability, trust, data access, defensibility, or enterprise sales cycles?**

7. **What single proof in a 24-hour demo would change your reaction from “voice-agent wrapper” to “venture-worthy system”?**

8. **If judges remember only one number and one before-and-after moment, what should they be?**

#### Ask if time remains

9. **Which business model best solves low consumer frequency: success fee, per-case B2B pricing, subscription, or white-label distribution?**

10. **Which impact layer feels commercially credible rather than performative: multilingual access, support for low-income users, older users, or accessibility needs?**

11. **What should we remove from the product or pitch immediately?**

12. **Which buyer profile should we interview next, and would you be comfortable making one narrowly scoped introduction if there is a genuine fit?**

### Do not spend the session on

- explaining Hack-Nation or reading the challenge brief;
- comparing models, frameworks, or the detailed tech stack;
- general AI trends;
- open-ended “What do you think?” questions;
- brainstorming another long list of verticals;
- debating top-down market size before identifying the buyer;
- asking broadly for introductions;
- showing more than one minute of the demo;
- defending every assumption instead of listening.

### Strong closing

> Let me play back the decision we heard: our first wedge is __; the buyer is __; the proof we need is __; and our next interview should be with __. Is that an accurate summary? What is the one thing you would do in our next two hours?

Do not end with “Any other advice?” End with a confirmed decision and one concrete next action.
